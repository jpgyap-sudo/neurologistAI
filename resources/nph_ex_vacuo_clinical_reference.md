# Clinical Reference Notes: NPH vs Hydrocephalus Ex Vacuo

## NPH mechanism
- Usually communicating hydrocephalus.
- Not typically a visible focal CSF blockage.
- Related to impaired CSF absorption and altered compliance.
- CT/MRI may show ventriculomegaly, tight high-convexity sulci, enlarged Sylvian fissures, and DESH.

## Ex vacuo mechanism
- Ventricular enlargement from brain volume loss.
- CSF passively occupies space left by atrophy, infarct, hemorrhage, encephalomalacia, or surgery.
- Shunt does not restore tissue.
- Overdrainage may cause brain sagging or subdural hematoma, especially in atrophic brains.

## Key radiologic terms
- **Tight high-convexity sulci**: supports NPH/DESH.
- **Widened sulci**: supports atrophy/ex vacuo.
- **DESH**: disproportionate enlarged subarachnoid-space hydrocephalus; enlarged Sylvian fissures with tight vertex sulci.
- **Callosal angle**: smaller angles are more typical of NPH; larger angles often favor atrophy/ex vacuo, but must be measured correctly on coronal images.
- **Evans index**: screens for ventriculomegaly but does not distinguish NPH from ex vacuo by itself.
- **Periventricular FLAIR/CT hypodensity**: may represent transependymal CSF flow in hydrocephalus, but chronic gliosis/leukoaraiosis/stroke injury can mimic it.

## CSF testing
- Tap test: improvement after removal supports NPH; no improvement lowers probability of shunt benefit.
- Repeated negative LP has more weight in decision-making.
- CSF infusion test/Rout is more objective for CSF absorption resistance.

## Shunt risk concepts
- Infection
- Overdrainage
- Subdural hematoma
- Valve setting error after MRI
- Revision or blockage
- Management burden

## Practical safety principle
In suspected ex vacuo, if shunt is done despite uncertainty, use a programmable valve with conservative high starting pressure and anti-siphon/gravitational protection, as determined by neurosurgery.
