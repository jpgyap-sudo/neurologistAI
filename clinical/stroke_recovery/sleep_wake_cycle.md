# Sleep–Wake Cycle Management After Severe Brain Injury

## Circadian Disruption in Severe Brain Injury

- Hypothalamic and brainstem pathways regulating circadian rhythm are frequently disrupted after stroke, TBI, or hemorrhage.
- Manifestations: daytime somnolence, nighttime agitation, fragmented sleep, inverted cycles.
- Sleep disruption impairs neuroplasticity, delays rehabilitation participation, and confounds consciousness assessments.

## Melatonin

| Parameter | Guidance |
|-----------|----------|
| Dose | 0.5–5 mg at bedtime |
| Timing | Administer 30–60 minutes before desired sleep time; keep consistent nightly |
| Formulation | Immediate-release preferred for sleep onset; extended-release if frequent awakenings |
| Titration | Start low (0.5–1 mg) and increase by 1 mg every 3–5 days if no response |

- Melatonin is generally well tolerated and has minimal interaction with anticoagulants or antihypertensives.
- Use cautiously in patients with seizure disorders; evidence on proconvulsant risk is mixed but theoretical concern exists.

## Light Therapy

- Bright light exposure (≥2500 lux) for 30 minutes in the morning (ideally within 1 hour of waking).
- Goal: anchor circadian phase, improve nighttime sleep consolidation, and increase daytime alertness.
- Combine with opening blinds, moving patient to a naturally lit area, or using a light box if available.

## Sleep Hygiene for Inpatient Setting

- Maintain consistent sleep and wake times.
- Minimize nighttime interruptions: cluster vital signs, medication passes, and nursing care.
- Reduce noise and light exposure after 8 PM (use dim lighting, close doors).
- Avoid caffeine after midday.
- Encourage daytime activity and therapy sessions to build sleep pressure.
- Limit naps to <30 minutes and before 3 PM.

## Pharmacologic Cautions

- **Avoid sedating antihistamines** (e.g., diphenhydramine, promethazine): anticholinergic burden, delirium risk, and paradoxical agitation, especially in elderly or brain-injured patients.
- If non-pharmacologic measures fail and severe sleep disruption persists, consider low-dose trazodone (25–50 mg at bedtime) or low-dose mirtazapine (7.5 mg), balancing sedation benefit against anticholinergic or weight-gain side effects.
- Avoid benzodiazepines for sleep in brain-injured patients due to cognitive suppression and paradoxical agitation risk.

## Safety

- Monitor for paradoxical agitation or worsening confusion after initiating melatonin or any sleep aid.
- Re-evaluate sleep–wake cycle weekly; correlate with rehabilitation engagement and family reports.
- Document environmental factors (ICU noise, frequent neuro checks) that may override pharmacologic interventions.
