# Pain Assessment in Stroke Recovery

## Challenges in Disorders of Consciousness (DoC) and Minimally Conscious Patients

- Self-report is unreliable or impossible.
- Motor responses may be inconsistent; pain behaviors can be misattributed to agitation or reflex.
- Prolonged immobility increases risk for nociceptive and neuropathic sources that go undetected.

## Validated Assessment Scales

| Scale | Population | Domains assessed | Scoring |
|-------|-----------|------------------|---------|
| NCS-R (Nociception Coma Scale-Revised) | DoC / vegetative and minimally conscious states | Motor, verbal, facial expression | 0–9; ≥4 suggests significant nociception |
| PAINAD (Pain Assessment in Advanced Dementia) | Cognitive impairment / non-verbal patients | Breathing, vocalization, facial expression, body language, consolability | 0–10; ≥3 warrants analgesic trial |
| Doloplus-2 | Elderly / cognitively impaired | Somatic, psychomotor, psychosocial reactions | 0–30; ≥5 suggests pain |

- Use the same scale serially; consistency of raters improves reliability.
- If uncertain, conduct a time-limited analgesic trial and reassess behavior.

## Common Pain Sources in Stroke Recovery

| Source | Clues | Evaluation |
|--------|-------|------------|
| Spasticity | Increased tone, triggering with movement, focal tenderness | Ashworth/Tardieu scale; response to stretching |
| Heterotopic ossification | Warmth, swelling, decreased ROM around hip, knee, elbow | X-ray or ultrasound; elevated alkaline phosphatase |
| Pressure injury | Erythema over bony prominences, non-blanching, stageable lesions | Skin inspection with offloading; document stage per NPUAP |
| Occult fracture | Localized swelling, deformity, pain on passive movement after fall or seizure | X-ray; if negative and high suspicion, CT or MRI |
| Shoulder subluxation / complex regional pain syndrome | Swelling, discoloration, allodynia in hemiplegic limb | Clinical exam; bone scan if CRPS suspected |

## Analgesic Ladder with Neuro-Specific Cautions

**Step 1: Non-opioid analgesics**
- Acetaminophen: first line; avoid exceeding 3 g/day in hepatic impairment or chronic alcohol use.
- NSAIDs: use with caution in patients with ICH history, renal impairment, or anticoagulation; avoid in post-hemorrhagic stroke if bleeding risk is not fully resolved.

**Step 2: Adjuvants for neuropathic pain**
- Gabapentin: 100–300 mg TID, titrate to effect; monitor sedation.
- Pregabalin: 25–75 mg BID, titrate; less renal adjustment needed than gabapentin at low doses but still renally cleared.
- Tricyclic antidepressants (amitriptyline, nortriptyline): effective but anticholinergic and sedating; avoid in patients with cardiac conduction abnormalities.

**Step 3: Opioids**
- Short-acting agents for acute, severe pain (e.g., oxycodone 2.5–5 mg PRN).
- **Caution:** Opioids can oversedate, suppress respiratory drive, and confound neurological examinations. In DoC patients, prefer the lowest effective dose and reassess arousal frequently.
- Morphine may be useful in refractory autonomic storming (see `autonomic_dysregulation.md`), but monitor for hypotension and ileus.

## Non-Pharmacologic Strategies

- **Positioning:** Offload heels, sacrum, and occiput; use pillows to support hemiplegic limb and prevent shoulder traction.
- **Orthotics:** AFOs or resting splints to reduce noxious joint stress from spasticity.
- **TENS:** Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation over painful muscle groups or dermatomes; avoid over areas with impaired sensation or open wounds.

## Safety

- Analgesic trials should be time-limited with scheduled reassessment (e.g., 30–60 minutes post-dose).
- Avoid agents that cloud consciousness when the primary goal is neurorecovery or assessment of consciousness level.
- Document pain source whenever identified; do not treat pain as an isolated symptom without investigation.
