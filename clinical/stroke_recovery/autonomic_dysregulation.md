# Autonomic Dysregulation After Stroke

## Sympathetic Hyperactivity (Storming)

**Common triggers**
- Noxious stimuli: urinary retention, constipation, infection, pain, pressure areas.
- Environmental: loud noise, abrupt repositioning, endotracheal suctioning.
- Medical: fever, hypoxia, seizures, deep vein thrombosis.

**Signs**
- Tachycardia (>120 bpm or >2 SD above baseline).
- Hypertension (systolic BP >160 mmHg or widened pulse pressure).
- Hyperthermia (>38.3°C) without proven infection.
- Diaphoresis, flushing, pupillary dilation.
- Posturing (often extensor).

## Medications for Sympathetic Storming

| Agent | Typical dosing | Rationale / caveat |
|-------|---------------|--------------------|
| Propranolol | 10–40 mg PO q6–8h; titrate to HR <120 and SBP <160 | Non-selective beta-blockade; effective for tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis |
| Clonidine | 0.1 mg PO BID–TID; patch 0.1 mg/24h | Central alpha-2 agonist; watch for hypotension and rebound hypertension if stopped abruptly |
| Gabapentin | 300 mg TID, titrate to 1200–1800 mg/day | Modulates autonomic outflow; useful if storming is refractory to beta-blockade |
| Baclofen | 5–10 mg TID, titrate as tolerated | GABA-B agonist; reduces motor storming and autonomic surges; may add to oral regimen or consider intrathecal |
| Morphine | 2–5 mg IV q2–4h PRN | Blunts catecholamine surge; reserve for severe, refractory episodes; monitor respiratory drive |

- Start with a single agent; escalate to combination therapy if episodes persist >24–48 hours.
- Morphine is reserved for acute, severe paroxysms unresponsive to first-line agents.

## Autonomic Dysreflexia in Spinal-Cord-Involved Stroke

- Occurs in patients with cervical or high thoracic lesions (T6 and above).
- Triggered by bladder distension, bowel impaction, or skin irritation below the level of injury.
- Presentation: sudden severe hypertension, bradycardia, headache, diaphoresis, flushing.
- **Immediate management:**
  1. Sit patient upright (if spinal precautions permit) to lower cerebral perfusion pressure.
  2. Identify and remove the stimulus (check Foley, bowel, skin).
  3. If SBP remains >150 mmHg, give rapid-onset antihypertensive (e.g., nifedipine 10 mg bite-and-swallow, or hydralazine 10–20 mg IV).
  4. Monitor BP every 2–5 minutes until stabilized.

## Non-Pharmacologic Management

- **Environmental control:** quiet room, dim lights, minimal handling during episodes.
- **Bowel and bladder regimen:** scheduled toileting, avoid retention; monitor I/O closely.
- **Skin checks:** every 2 hours for pressure injuries or noxious stimuli in insensate areas.
- **Temperature management:** active cooling (fans, cooling blankets) for hyperthermia; avoid shivering.

## Safety

- **Beta-blockers (especially propranolol)** can mask hypoglycemia symptoms (tachycardia, tremors). Monitor glucose regularly in diabetic patients or those on insulin.
- **Clonidine** can cause hypotension, sedation, and rebound hypertension on withdrawal—taper if discontinuing.
- Do not attribute all fever to autonomic storming; maintain a low threshold to evaluate for infection (urine, blood, sputum cultures; chest imaging).
