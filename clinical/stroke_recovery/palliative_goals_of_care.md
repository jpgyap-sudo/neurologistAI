# Palliative Care and Goals of Care in Severe Stroke

## Prognostication in Severe Stroke and Disorders of Consciousness (DoC)

| Scale | Levels | Interpretation |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| Glasgow Outcome Scale–Extended (GOSE) | 1 (dead) to 8 (upper good recovery) | GOSE ≤4 at 6 months predicts severe disability; GOSE ≥5 indicates moderate recovery or better |
| Cerebral Performance Category (CPC) | 1 (good) to 5 (brain death) | CPC 3 = severe disability (conscious but dependent); CPC 4 = coma / vegetative state |

- Prognosis is most reliable when assessed after withdrawal of sedation and after 72 hours post-injury.
- Multimodal assessment improves accuracy: clinical exam (GCS, CRS-R), neuroimaging (DWI MRI, quantitative volume loss), and electrophysiology (EEG reactivity, SSEPs).
- Avoid premature prognostication in the first 24–72 hours; metabolic, toxic, and pharmacologic confounders are common.

## Shared Decision-Making Frameworks

- Identify the legal surrogate decision-maker (healthcare proxy, durable power of attorney, or next-of-kin per local law).
- Elicit patient values: prior expressed wishes, religious beliefs, tolerance for dependency, and views on life-sustaining therapy.
- Present prognosis honestly but with appropriate uncertainty; avoid statements that imply certainty (e.g., “will never wake up”).
- Offer balanced options: full code with aggressive interventions, limited interventions with comfort-focused goals, or transition to comfort care only.
- Document the surrogate, the discussion, and the agreed-upon goals in the medical record.

## Advance Directive Review

- Verify existence of living will, advance directive, or POLST/MOLST forms.
- If the patient lacks capacity and no directive exists, follow institutional policy for surrogate consent.
- Revisit code status and goals at key transition points: ICU admission, tracheostomy consideration, PEG placement, discharge to long-term care, or clinical deterioration.

## Hospice Criteria for End-Stage Neurological Disease

- Rapidly declining functional status (declining GOSE or CPC over weeks).
- Severe dysphagia with recurrent aspiration or refusal of artificial nutrition/hydration.
- Recurrent infections (pneumonia, urosepsis) despite treatment.
- Significant weight loss (>10% in 6 months) unrelated to reversible causes.
- Dependence in all activities of daily living with minimal or no meaningful interaction.
- Hospice eligibility varies by region and payer; involve palliative care consultation early to navigate criteria.

## Withdrawing and Withholding Life-Sustaining Therapy

| Consideration | Guidance |
|---------------|----------|
| Ethics | Withholding and withdrawing are ethically equivalent when they align with patient values or best-interest standard for incapacitated patients. |
| Legal | Follow jurisdictional law regarding surrogate authority, mandatory waiting periods, and documentation requirements. |
| Process | Convene family conference; include nursing, social work, chaplaincy, and palliative care. Provide clear timeline. |
| Symptom management | Pre-treat with opioids (e.g., morphine 2–5 mg IV) and benzodiazepines for air hunger and anxiety; titrate to comfort after extubation. |

## Safety

- Ensure cultural sensitivity: beliefs about death, disability, and medical intervention vary widely; ask rather than assume.
- Involve ethics consultation when there is conflict among family members, between family and care team, or when surrogate decisions appear inconsistent with known patient values.
- Do not rush decisions in the setting of acute grief or shock; offer follow-up meetings and written summaries.
- Protect patient dignity throughout; privacy and family presence should be accommodated per family wishes.
