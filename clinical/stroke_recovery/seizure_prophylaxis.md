# Seizure Prophylaxis After Stroke

⚠️ This document is clinical decision support. It does not replace physician judgment or institutional protocols.

This is decision support, not a prescription directive.

## Post-Stroke Seizure Risk Factors

| Factor | Risk Implication |
|--------|-----------------|
| Hemorrhagic stroke (ICH, SAH) | Higher early seizure risk than ischemic stroke |
| Cortical involvement / lobar location | Increased epileptogenic potential |
| Large infarct / hematoma volume | Greater tissue injury and excitotoxicity |
| Severe NIHSS / depressed consciousness | Correlates with structural burden |
| Prior seizures | Strongest predictor of recurrence |
| Younger age | Some evidence of higher susceptibility |

- Early seizures (<7 days): often related to acute metabolic/ionic derangement or direct irritation.
- Late seizures (>7 days): suggest gliosis / scar formation; may warrant longer-term management.

## Prophylaxis Indications and Contra-Indications

### Indications (individualize per team)
- Lobar intracerebral hemorrhage
- Subarachnoid hemorrhage with cortical blood
- Large cortical ischemic stroke with severe edema
- Documented early seizure with high recurrence risk
- Post-craniotomy / decompressive hemicraniectomy

### Contra-Indications / Cautions
- Known hypersensitivity to agent
- Severe hepatic impairment (valproate, phenytoin)
- Severe renal impairment (dose-adjust levetiracetam)
- History of bone marrow suppression or Stevens-Johnson syndrome
- Concurrent sedating medications that impair neuro-monitoring

## Common Agents

### Levetiracetam
- **Typical dosing**: 500–1000 mg PO/IV q12h; load 1000–1500 mg if urgent coverage needed
- **Renal**: Adjust per CrCl (extended interval or reduced dose if <80 mL/min)
- **Side effects**: Somnolence, dizziness, behavioral changes, thrombocytopenia
- **Advantages**: Minimal drug–drug interactions; preferred in most neuro-ICU settings

### Phenytoin
- **Typical dosing**: Load 15–20 mg/kg IV; maintenance 5 mg/kg/day divided q8–12h
- **Monitoring**: Free/total phenytoin levels; albumin correction if hypoalbuminemia
- **Side effects**: Hypotension with rapid IV infusion, arrhythmias, gingival hyperplasia, rash/SJS, enzyme induction
- **Cautions**: Numerous drug interactions; falling out of favor for prophylaxis

### Valproate
- **Typical dosing**: Load 15–20 mg/kg IV; maintenance 10–15 mg/kg/day divided q8–12h
- **Monitoring**: LFTs, ammonia, CBC, platelets
- **Side effects**: Hepatotoxicity, pancreatitis, hyperammonemia, tremor, weight gain, teratogenicity
- **Cautions**: Avoid in pregnancy; caution with hepatic dysfunction and enzyme inhibitors

## When to Discontinue Prophylaxis
- If given purely for primary prophylaxis without seizure occurrence:
  - Reassess at 7–14 days.
  - Discontinue if no seizure, improving exam, and low residual risk per neuro team.
- If seizure occurred:
  - Continue at least until the acute phase resolves and imaging stabilizes.
  - Transition to oral agent when enteral access and absorption are reliable.
  - Re-evaluate need at 3–6 months based on EEG findings and etiology.

## References / Evidence
- AHA/ASA Guidelines for Management of Spontaneous Intracerebral Hemorrhage (seizure management recommendations).
- Cochrane reviews: limited evidence supports routine long-term prophylaxis after stroke without seizures.
