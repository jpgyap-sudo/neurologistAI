# Spasticity Management After Stroke

## Assessment Scales

| Scale | What it measures | Key thresholds |
|-------|-----------------|--------------|
| Modified Ashworth Scale (MAS) | Resistance to passive stretch in specific muscle groups | 0 = no increase; 4 = affected part rigid in flexion/extension |
| Tardieu Scale | Quality of muscle reaction (spastic vs contractile) at different velocities | “Catch” angle at fast velocity vs slow velocity; angle difference >10° suggests spasticity |

- Assess upper extremity (finger flexors, wrist flexors, elbow flexors) and lower extremity (hip adductors, knee extensors, ankle plantarflexors).
- Re-evaluate after each intervention to guide dosing or referral.

## Oral Agents

| Agent | Typical dose | Key cautions |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| Baclofen | 5 mg TID, titrate to 20 mg TID (max 80 mg/day) | Sedation, weakness, hypotension; withdrawal can precipitate seizures—taper slowly |
| Tizanidine | 2–4 mg at bedtime, titrate to 8 mg TID | Hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs); additive sedation with CNS depressants |
| Dantrolene | 25 mg daily, titrate to 100 mg TID | Hepatotoxicity; weakness; use when spasticity involves multiple muscle groups and ITB is not an option |
| Benzodiazepines (e.g., diazepam, clonazepam) | Short-term, lowest effective dose | Significant sedation, cognitive slowing, respiratory depression; avoid in patients with disorders of consciousness |

- Start one agent; titrate before adding a second.
- Avoid stacking multiple GABAergic drugs (baclofen + benzodiazepine) because of oversedation risk.

## Focal Treatments: Botulinum Toxin

**Indications**
- Focal spasticity causing pain, hygiene difficulties, or functional impairment.
- Common targets: wrist/finger flexors, biceps, adductor magnus, gastrocnemius/soleus.

**Dosing Zones (rough guide per muscle; follow product-specific maximums)**

| Muscle | Typical total units (onabotulinumtoxinA) |
|--------|------------------------------------------|
| Biceps brachii | 100–200 U |
| Wrist flexors (FCR, FCU) | 50–100 U each |
| Finger flexors (FDS, FDP) | 25–50 U each |
| Adductor magnus/longus | 200–500 U |
| Gastrocnemius | 100–300 U per head |
| Soleus | 50–150 U |

- Use electromyography or ultrasound guidance for deep or small muscles.
- Expected onset: 3–7 days; peak effect: 2–4 weeks; duration: 10–16 weeks.
- Re-inject no sooner than 12 weeks.

## Intrathecal Baclofen (ITB) Pump

**Candidacy**
- Severe generalized spasticity (often MAS ≥3 in multiple limbs) causing pain, positioning difficulty, or caregiver burden.
- Intolerance to or failure of oral agents.
- No active infection; adequate abdominal wall for pump pocket.

**Trial Dosing**
- Lumbar intrathecal test dose (usually 50 mcg baclofen); observe for 4–8 hours.
- Document change in Ashworth score, pain, and ease of care.
- A positive trial (≥1-point drop in Ashworth in targeted limbs) supports implantation.

**Complications**
- Catheter malfunction, infection, CSF leak, baclofen withdrawal (can be life-threatening; treat with high-dose oral/enteral baclofen if pump access is delayed).

## Physical Modalities

- **Stretching:** Daily range-of-motion to antagonist muscles; hold 30–60 seconds.
- **Splinting:** Resting hand splints, ankle–foot orthoses (AFOs) to maintain functional position and prevent contracture.
- **Serial casting:** For ankle plantarflexion contractures; change cast every 3–7 days with progressive dorsiflexion.
- **Functional electrical stimulation (FES):** Cyclic or task-specific stimulation to antagonist muscles; may reduce spasticity indirectly through reciprocal inhibition.

## Safety Considerations

- Do not let spasticity treatment mask occult pain, fracture, or heterotopic ossification.
- Monitor joints at risk (shoulder, wrist, ankle) for contracture development at least weekly in immobile patients.
- Document goals of spasticity treatment (comfort vs function) to avoid overtreatment causing weakness.
