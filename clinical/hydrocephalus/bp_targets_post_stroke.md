# Blood Pressure Targets After Stroke

## Post-Intracerebral Hemorrhage (ICH) Management

| Phase | SBP Goal | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Acute (first 24 hours) | <140–160 mmHg (institutional protocol may vary) | AHA/ASA guidelines suggest SBP <160 is reasonable; some centers target <140 if ICP is not elevated |
| Subacute (24 hours to 7 days) | <140 mmHg | Prevent hematoma expansion; avoid hypotension that could reduce perihematomal perfusion |
| Permissive hypertension window | May allow SBP up to 180–220 mmHg if cerebral autoregulation is impaired or elevated ICP is present | Requires close neuro-monitoring; shorten permissive window if imaging shows mass effect or hydrocephalus |

- Do not lower SBP abruptly; aim for smooth, controlled reduction over hours.
- Monitor neurologic exam during titration; any decline should prompt reassessment of target and evaluation for hydrocephalus or rebleeding.

## Post-Ischemic Stroke Management

| Parameter | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Systolic BP | <130 mmHg |
| Diastolic BP | <80 mmHg |

- Achieve this target within days to weeks after the acute period, provided there are no contraindications (e.g., bilateral carotid stenosis, intracranial stenosis requiring higher perfusion pressures).
- Recurrent stroke risk increases with sustained BP >130/80; however, avoid overly aggressive lowering in the first 24–48 hours after large-territory infarct due to risk of worsening cerebral edema.

## Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP) Considerations

- **Target CPP:** 60–70 mmHg in patients with elevated ICP or impaired autoregulation.
  - CPP = MAP − ICP
  - If ICP is not monitored, use clinical surrogates (mental status, pupillary exam) and imaging to infer adequacy.
- **Avoid hypotension** in the setting of elevated ICP; even brief drops in MAP can precipitate secondary ischemic injury.
- In patients with decompressive craniectomy, autoregulation may be disturbed; maintain MAP ≥80 mmHg until bone flap replacement.

## Antihypertensive Agent Selection in Neuro Patients

| Agent | Route | Dosing | Advantages | Cautions |
|-------|-------|--------|------------|----------|
| Labetalol | IV | 10–20 mg bolus, then 2–8 mg/min infusion | Non-selective beta + alpha-1 blockade; does not raise ICP | Avoid in asthma, heart block, acute heart failure; can cause hypotension if pushed too fast |
| Nicardipine | IV infusion | 5 mg/hr, titrate by 2.5 mg/hr q5–15 min (max 15 mg/hr) | Predictable titration; minimal effect on ICP | Reflex tachycardia; hypotension if titrated aggressively |
| Clevidipine | IV infusion | 1–2 mg/hr, titrate by doubling q90 sec (max 21 mg/hr) | Ultra-short acting; rapid on/off; useful in neurocritical care | Contraindicated in soy/egg allergy; lipid emulsion increases caloric load; monitor triglycerides |
| Hydralazine | IV/IM | 10–20 mg q4–6h PRN | Useful when beta-blockade is contraindicated | Unpredictable response; reflex tachycardia; less preferred in neuro patients |

- **Avoid oral agents in acute neurocritical care** until hemodynamics are stable and enteral access is reliable.
- Transition from IV to oral antihypertensives once neurologic status is stable for 24 hours and SBP remains consistently <140 mmHg.

## Safety

- Re-evaluate BP targets daily in the context of ICP trends, imaging (evolving edema, hydrocephalus, hematoma expansion), and clinical exam.
- In patients with known carotid or intracranial stenosis, individualize targets to avoid hypoperfusion; consider permissive hypertension (SBP 140–180) until revascularization or stenosis is addressed.
- Document the BP target range and the clinical rationale when deviating from standard goals.
